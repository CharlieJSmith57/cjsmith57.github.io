Overview

This project involved redesigning the oil-to-air heat exchanger for an aerospace auxiliary power unit (APU) operating in high-altitude environments. The original design was oversized for its envelope and showed poor thermal uniformity, leading to localized hotspots and reduced component life.

The objective was to improve overall thermal efficiency while reducing the unit volume by 30% to meet an updated packaging envelope — a constrained multi-objective problem requiring tight coordination between CFD analysis and manufacturing feasibility.

Approach

I began with a parametric CFD study in ANSYS Fluent to characterize the existing design's performance. Conjugate heat transfer simulations with a realizable k-ε turbulence model revealed a pronounced recirculation zone on the shell side that was responsible for the thermal non-uniformity observed in field measurements.

A baffle redesign study followed — varying baffle cut percentage, spacing, and orientation across a Latin Hypercube sample of 64 design points. Response surface models were fit to the NTU and pressure drop outputs, and a Pareto front was extracted balancing the two competing objectives. The selected design used helical baffles at 35° with a 25% cut, which suppressed the recirculation while maintaining acceptable shell-side pressure drop.

All candidate designs were validated with full-fidelity CFD before the selected geometry was submitted for ASME BPVC Section VIII stress analysis and prototype manufacturing.

Outcome

The redesigned heat exchanger achieved a 22% improvement in thermal effectiveness (ε from 0.61 to 0.74) and fit within the new packaging envelope with a 31% volume reduction. The peak wall temperature hotspot was reduced by 14°C, extending predicted component life by a factor of 2.3 based on Arrhenius degradation modeling.

The unit passed qualification testing including altitude chamber simulation to 40,000 ft and 500-cycle thermal fatigue testing.
