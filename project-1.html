Overview

The goal of this project was to redesign an existing steel monocoque chassis for a light electric vehicle — reducing mass aggressively while maintaining structural compliance with all applicable FMVSS safety standards. The original design weighed 187 kg and had significant material redundancy identified through initial FEA runs.

Working as the lead structural engineer, I led the full redesign from load case definition through prototype build verification, coordinating with manufacturing partners on DFM constraints throughout.

Approach

We started by establishing a comprehensive load case matrix: static bending, torsion, side impact (FMVSS 214), roof crush (FMVSS 216), and combined dynamic road loads derived from measured PSD data. These were used as objective and constraint inputs into the topology optimization solver.

Three design iterations were run in Altair OptiStruct with progressively tighter mass targets. The optimizer consistently suggested a hybrid space-frame geometry — ribs of material retained at predictable load paths with large voids elsewhere. We then interpreted these organic outputs into manufacturable geometry, converting to an aluminum extrusion and welded space-frame construction.

All weld joints were individually validated in ANSYS with sub-model mesh refinement. Fatigue life was estimated using Goodman criteria with a target of 200,000 km equivalent road loading.

34% Mass Reduction
187→124 kg (Before → After)
1.8× Safety Factor (Crush)

Outcome

The final chassis passed all physical validation tests including a quasi-static roof crush fixture test and a side-pole impact sled run. Mass came in at 124 kg — a 34% reduction from baseline — while achieving a 1.8× safety factor on roof crush, exceeding the FMVSS 216 requirement.

The manufacturing cost per unit also decreased by approximately 18% due to simplified joining operations enabled by the space-frame architecture, compared to the stamping tooling required for the original monocoque design.
