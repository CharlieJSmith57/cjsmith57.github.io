Overview

As part of WPI's Great Problems Seminar (a program that introduces first-year students to WPI's project-based learning curriculum) I co-authored a research plan focused on delivering clean drinking water to the town of Abalessa in the Tamanrasset Province of southern Algeria, a remote and impoverished region of the Sahara Desert.

Access to fresh water is one of the most critical and persistent challenges facing communities in this arid region. Abalessa faces extreme heat, near-zero annual rainfall, and limited infrastructure. Making conventional water delivery and well-based systems unreliable or infeasible.

The Technology

Our research centered on Zeolite-based atmospheric water generation (AWG), a technology developed by researchers at MIT. Zeolites are microporous aluminosilicate minerals with an exceptionally high surface area and strong hygroscopic properties. This makes them excellent at passively absorbing water vapor directly from ambient air, even in low-humidity environments like the Sahara.

Unlike traditional AWG systems that require electricity and mechanical refrigeration to condense moisture, Zeolite-based collectors can operate passively using the natural temperature differential between night and day. During cooler nighttime hours, the Zeolite material adsorbs atmospheric humidity; during the heat of the day, the stored water is released and collected. This passive, off-grid operation makes it particularly well-suited to remote, resource-limited communities.

Research Plan

Our co-authored research plan outlined the feasibility of deploying a Zeolite AWG array scaled to meet the daily drinking water needs of Abalessa's population. The plan addressed site-specific climate conditions (temperature range, relative humidity data), estimated daily water yield per unit area of Zeolite material, logistical considerations for manufacturing and deploying collector arrays, and community integration strategies.

The project required synthesizing sources across materials science, humanitarian engineering, regional geography, and development economics — developing skills in cross-disciplinary technical research and clear scientific communication under the GPS program framework.

Outcome

The research plan was completed and presented as part of the WPI Great Problems Seminar program. The project developed my ability to approach open-ended, real-world engineering problems with limited constraints — identifying appropriate technologies, evaluating feasibility, and communicating findings clearly. It was my first formal exposure to applying engineering thinking to humanitarian challenges, and it reinforced my interest in sustainable and practical engineering solutions.
